When it comes to building a lens kit, plenty of photographers
aimlessly purchase equipment with little strategy in mind.

Unfortunately, camera lenses can be expensive, making it less
practical in many cases to simply add a new lens to your kit
willy-nilly.

=E2=80=8B
That is exactly why we=E2=80=99ve written this... for the dedicated
photographer who wants to build the best lens kit for their craft
while also keeping in mind their needs, their current skill
level, and, perhaps most importantly, their budget.

* Check your metadata before updating.=E2=80=8B
If you have no idea which type of lens you use most, take a look
at your Lightroom metadata.
=E2=80=8B
This will give you plenty of information about which lenses you
utilize, something that can play a huge role in guiding you
toward future purchases!
=E2=80=8B
* Remember that the subject comes first.=E2=80=8B
Ask yourself what kinds of subjects you currently photograph or
want to photograph in the future. If you shoot mostly portrait
photography, a 50mm lens may be the right choice for you.
=E2=80=8B
If you=E2=80=99re interested in the stunning potential of shooting bugs
and flowers, consider a macro lens.
=E2=80=8B
Landscape photography tends to benefit from a wide-angle lens,
while sports and wildlife genres encourage the use of a telephoto
lens.
=E2=80=8B
* Consider renting before making a huge decision.=E2=80=8B
If, even after extensive consideration, you still find yourself
uncertain about which lenses to add to your lens kit, save
yourself a lot of money by renting a potential new lens first.
=E2=80=8B
Try it out for a while and see how you like it. Then, if you find
that the lens you=E2=80=99re renting fills a gap in your kit, invest.
=E2=80=8B
Finally, remember that one size does not fit all. Because every
photographer has different priorities, you may have your own
additional plan for a lens kit build that we haven=E2=80=99t mentioned
here.
=E2=80=8B
If that=E2=80=99s the case, who are we to stop you?
