<p><span style="color:#4e5f70;"><span style="font-size:8px;">A unique species of flying reptile, or pterosaur, that lived 168-166 million years ago has been discovered on the Isle of Skye.</span></span></p>





<p><span style="color:#4e5f70;"><span style="font-size:8px;">Its wings, shoulders, legs and backbone were found in a rock on a beach, but the fossil&#39;s skull was missing.</span></span></p>





<p><span style="color:#4e5f70;"><span style="font-size:8px;">Scientists were surprised to find a pterosaur from this period - they were thought to mostly live in China.</span></span></p>





<p><span style="color:#4e5f70;"><span style="font-size:8px;">The creature - called Ceoptera - is the second pterosaur found on Skye.</span></span></p>





<p><span style="color:#4e5f70;"><span style="font-size:8px;">Its name comes from the Scottish Gaelic word che&ograve;, meaning mist, and is a reference to the Gaelic name of Isle of Skye - Eilean a&#39; Che&ograve; or Isle of Mist.</span></span></p>





<p><span style="color:#4e5f70;"><span style="font-size:8px;">Fossils from this era, called the Middle Jurassic, are extremely rare, says Dr Liz Martin-Silverstone at the University of Bristol who used a CT scanner to make a 3D digital model of the fossil.</span></span></p>





<p><span style="color:#4e5f70;"><span style="font-size:8px;">&quot;Finding anything from that time period that&#39;s more than just a single bone is really exciting,&quot; she says.</span></span></p>





<p><span style="color:#4e5f70;"><span style="font-size:8px;">The creature probably had a wing-span of around 1m to 1.5m.</span></span></p>





<p><span style="color:#4e5f70;"><span style="font-size:8px;">Prof Steve Brusatte, who was not involved in the research, says it was unique to Scotland. The research proves that a type of pterosaur between the primitive and advanced stages of evolution existed, he adds.</span></span></p>





<p><span style="color:#4e5f70;"><span style="font-size:8px;">&quot;This is the time before birds, so pterosaurs ruled the sky. This research shows that pterosaurs were common animals in Scotland, soaring over the heads of dinosaurs,&quot; he said.</span></span></p>
